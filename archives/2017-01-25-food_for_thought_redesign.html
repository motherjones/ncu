Exclusive to Newsletter Subscribers

"Sugar, more than anything, seems to have made life worth living (as it still does),

particularly for those whose lives were absent the kind of pleasures that relative wealth and daily hours of leisure might otherwise provide," writes Gary Taubes in his new book, The Case Against Sugar.

Taubes explores our early ancestors' relationship with the substance: "As early as the 12th century, one contemporary chronicler of the Crusades, Albert of Aachen, was describing merely the opportunity to sample the sugar from the cane that the Crusaders found growing in the fields of what are now Israel and Lebanon as in and of itself 'some compensation for the sufferings they had endured...The pilgrims,' he wrote, 'could not get enough of its sweetness.'"
